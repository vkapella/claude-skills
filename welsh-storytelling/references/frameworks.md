# Welsh Frameworks Reference

This is the authoritative reference for Michael Welsh's storytelling frameworks from *The Backstory on Storytelling*. Read this FIRST when the skill is triggered.

The frameworks fall into three groups: structural (shape the deck), delivery (shape the Story Guide), and philosophical (shape both).

---

## A. Structural frameworks (shape the deck)

### The 5 Slide Rule

Not literally 5 slides. A philosophy of reduction and forcing function.

- **The math:** A 60-minute meeting has ~47 minutes of usable presentation time after intros, settling, and Q&A. Don't fill all 47 minutes with slides — leave room for conversation.
- **The principle:** Every slide must justify its existence. If it doesn't pass "why would anyone care?" — cut it.
- **The default ceiling:** 5-7 core business slides for a 30-min slot. 7-10 for a 60-min slot. Anything beyond goes in an appendix.
- **The push-back:** When users request 30+ slides, propose 5-7 plus a deep appendix. Curate ruthlessly.

### The Story Arc — Setup → What → How → Why → Magic Wand

The 5-beat arc Welsh recommends as the default narrative spine.

| Beat | Purpose | Common failure |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Setup** | Establish the situation, the problem, the constraint. The BME of why this conversation exists. | Skipped entirely. Most decks start at What. |
| **What** | The core message. The proposal, the recommendation, the news. ONE sentence. | Hidden in a multi-bullet "overview" slide. |
| **How** | The proof. The mechanism. The technical or operational case. Apply Rule of 3. | Becomes a 12-row table when 3 rows would decide it. |
| **Why** | What it means for the audience. The strategic frame. The cost/benefit framed honestly. | Replaced with another features dump. |
| **Magic Wand** | End with wanting. Paint the picture of success. State the ask. | "Thank you, any questions?" |

### Beginnings, Middles, Ends (BMEs)

Most people start in the middle (presenting facts without context) or at the end (showing results without the journey). This is "aftermath storytelling."

- Force a real beginning: where did this start? What broke? What changed?
- The BME applies twice — to the deck overall AND to each individual slide.
- Speaker notes for every slide should articulate the BME of THAT slide so the presenter can speak the connective tissue between slides.

### 3 Is More Than Magic — The Rule of 3

The power of threes. Not magical, but neurologically reliable.

- **On every content slide:** even if 5 things are shown, the speaker speaks to 3.
- **In the structure:** three reasons, three options, three commitments, three risks.
- **In linkage:** each section connects back to the prior with a 3-beat reference.
- **Why not 4 or 5?** Audiences remember 3. They forget 5. They lose count past 7.

### Designing for Diamonds

A Diamond has five facets. The same truth seen from five angles.

| Facet | What it is | Where it lives in the deck |
|-------|-----------|----------------------------|
| **The Point** | The headline statement. One sentence. | The "What" slide, in a navy box, large type. |
| **Context** | The why behind the point. The setup. | The "Setup" slide. Echoed in speaker notes. |
| **Evidence** | The data, the proof. | The "How" slide, the technical case. |
| **Application** | What it means for the audience specifically. | The "Why" slide, the strategic frame. |
| **Call to Action** | What happens next. What you need from them. | The "Magic Wand" slide. |

In the Story Guide, the Diamond is laid out as a 5-row table so the presenter can pivot between facets when fielding questions. Asked about cost? Surface the Application facet. Asked about technical risk? Surface Evidence. Asked about timeline? Surface Call to Action.

### Arcs of Uncertainty

The five-stage arc for talks where the topic is genuinely uncertain (transformation pitches, leadership talks, post-mortems).

1. **Confidence** — "We knew exactly what we were doing." (often false)
2. **Dawning Realization** — "Something isn't going as planned."
3. **Trough of Despair** — The honest low point. The admission.
4. **Quiet Progress** — Small wins. Lessons. Course corrections.
5. **Emergent Understanding** — What we now know. What's still unknown.

Use this arc when the topic involves change, transformation, or honest uncertainty. The Trough of Despair is the most powerful slide in the deck — most decks delete it. Don't.

### Familiar → Foreign → Transformation → Return

The hero's journey applied to business storytelling. Use for innovation pitches, change management, technology transformation.

1. **Familiar** — Where we are now. The known world.
2. **Foreign** — What's changing in the world that disrupts the familiar.
3. **Transformation** — The proposal. The new capability. The bridge.
4. **Return** — The new familiar. Operating in the changed world.

### Linkage

A deck without linkage is a slide deck. A deck with linkage is a story.

- Each slide must explicitly set up the next.
- Speaker notes for each slide should end with "this leads us to..." for the presenter to actually say.
- No orphan slides — slides that don't connect to anything before or after get cut.

## B. Delivery frameworks (shape the Story Guide)

### Repetition with Variation (RWV) — 5 Principles

Tell the same truth multiple ways without sounding repetitive.

1. **Message Discipline** — Tell them what you'll tell them, tell them, tell them what you told them. The structure carries the message.
2. **Once Upon a Time Thinking** — Frame ideas as stories with characters, stakes, and time. "Two years ago, we tried X..." beats "X was attempted previously."
3. **Story Geometry** — Three angles, one story. Technical angle, financial angle, strategic angle — pick which one fits the listener.
4. **Mental Models as Variation Tools** — Analogies and metaphors give the same idea a different surface. "Think of it like X" lands when the literal explanation didn't.
5. **Complexity Simplified Through RWV** — Hard ideas need multiple passes from different directions. Don't explain once and move on.

In the Story Guide, build an RWV library: 3 ways to land each major message. The presenter picks 2-3 based on which angle the room responds to.

### Handling Curveball Questions — 6 Techniques

Welsh's playbook for hostile, unexpected, or trap questions.

1. **Don't react immediately.** Three-Second Rule before speaking.
2. **Acknowledge.** "That's an important question." (Don't be sycophantic — be brief.)
3. **Intentional silence.** Use the pause as a tool. It signals consideration.
4. **Characterize and Categorize.** "So if I'm hearing you correctly, you're asking whether [restated question]." Two purposes: confirms understanding AND buys thinking time.
5. **Disambiguate and Redirect.** "Help me understand the concern — is it about X or Y?" Forces the questioner to specify.
6. **Complete Disassembly.** Take the assumption inside the question apart. "There are three assumptions in that — let me address each."

In the Story Guide, draft 5-8 anticipated curveballs with:
- The technique tagged
- A drafted answer using that technique
- A note on when to use which

### Poise, Presence, Posture

The physical layer.

- **Box breathing (4-4-4-4):** Inhale 4 seconds, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Do four cycles before walking in.
- **Three-Second Rule:** Pause three full seconds before speaking after a question, after a slide change, after a tense moment.
- **Read the room:** Two leaning back? Slow down. Phones out? Skip ahead to the cost slide. Nodding? Land harder on the current point.
- **Move:** Don't hide behind the laptop. Step toward the audience on the most important point.
- **Eye contact:** One complete thought per person (3-5 seconds), then move on. Not the screen. Not the back wall.

### The Power of "I Don't Know"

Admitting uncertainty is the most underused move in business presentations.

- Vendors say "let me get back to you." Partners say "I don't know — let me think out loud about what I'd need to find out."
- "I don't know" turns a Q&A into a working session.
- The Story Guide should include an "I Don't Know" list — topics where the presenter is pre-authorized to admit uncertainty without scrambling.

## C. Philosophical principles (shape both deck AND guide)

These aren't frameworks with steps. They're operating principles.

### Start with no slides

Default state: zero slides. Each slide must pitch why it deserves to exist. This forces curation before content creation.

### Respect the audience's time

A presentation is "the gift of someone else's attention." Don't waste it. The 5 Slide Rule comes from this principle — fewer slides isn't laziness, it's respect.

### Curate ruthlessly

When stakeholders want their slide added, make them pitch why. The Story Guide includes a Cut List — content that was considered and removed, with reasons. This makes the curation visible and gives backup material if asked.

### The One Thing

Show up with genuine curiosity and a sincere desire to help. This is the meta-attitude that makes everything else work.

### Tell the truth

Don't lie, manipulate, hide behind jargon, or pretend to have answers you don't. The Power of "I Don't Know" is a corollary.

## When to use which arc

| If the deck is about... | Use this arc |
|---|---|
| A proposal, recommendation, or sales pitch | **5-Beat (Setup→What→How→Why→Magic Wand)** — default |
| Innovation, change management, or transformation | **Familiar → Foreign → Transformation → Return** |
| A post-mortem, leadership talk, or honest reflection | **Arc of Uncertainty** |
| A board update or status report | **5-Beat**, but compress Setup and expand Why |
| A technical deep-dive | **5-Beat**, but expand How and compress Why |

## Quick decision tree for slide count

- **Time slot ≤ 15 min:** 3-5 slides + appendix
- **Time slot 16-30 min:** 5-7 slides + appendix
- **Time slot 31-60 min:** 7-10 slides + appendix
- **Time slot 61+ min:** Section the deck and use Section Break slides; max 12 content slides per section

If the user requests dramatically more than these counts, push back. Welsh would.
